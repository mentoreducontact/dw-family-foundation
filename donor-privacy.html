Your generosity is not our asset.
It’s a responsibility we hold quietly.

When you give to DW Family Foundation, you fund a calm kit in a classroom, a tea after a trail, a story that helps a child name a hard feeling. You do not fund a mailing list. We built our donor privacy practice around one simple refusal: we will not trade your trust.

Why this page exists

A privacy policy tells you what we could do under law. A donor privacy promise tells you what we will never do, even if law would allow it. For a small family foundation, this is the entire fundraising ethic.

This commitment is aligned with the Donor Bill of Rights created by the Association of Fundraising Professionals, the Association for Healthcare Philanthropy, the Council for Advancement and Support of Education, and the Giving Institute. In particular, your right to be informed, to access your records, to be assured your gift is used as designated, and to expect that your personal information is held in confidence.

The lifecycle of a gift

To keep your trust tangible, here is what happens when you donate:

  1. You initiate: On our donate page or offline, you enter amount and contact details. Your browser talks directly to Stripe over TLS 1.2+.
  2. Processor validates: Stripe checks for fraud, verifies card or ACH, and returns a tokenized confirmation. Your full card number never touches our servers.
  3. We receive minimal receipt data: Name, email, last four digits, expiration month/year, amount, date, campaign name, and whether the gift is recurring. This is enough for accounting and a grateful email — nothing more.
  4. We steward: We issue a tax receipt, record the gift in a restricted-access ledger for IRS and audit compliance, and note your communication preferences.
  5. We retain only as needed: Seven years for accounting and nonprofit law, then minimized or deleted. See our Privacy Policy.

What we store vs what we never store

What we keep
  • Full name and email for receipt
  • Mailing address if you provide it for tax receipt
  • Amount, date, frequency, designation, campaign source
  • Last 4 digits + expiry month/year (from Stripe) for receipt help
  • Communication preference: email only, no mail, anonymous
  • Donation correspondence you initiate
What we never keep or collect
  • Full PAN, CVV, bank account numbers, PINs
  • Government IDs, wealth or income data
  • Third-party wealth screening, prospecting scores
  • Location tracking or advertising IDs
  • Donor data purchased from brokers
  • Information about children under 13 — our programs for 3-8 year olds go through parents only

What we never do

We make these negatives explicit because they are where trust is usually broken in philanthropy:

  • No sale, no trade, no rental. Donor names and contact information are not commodities.
  • No sharing for marketing. We do not give, lend, or transfer donor information to any other nonprofit, commercial entity, or political organization for their marketing or fundraising.
  • No mailings on behalf of others. We will not include inserts or send emails for other organizations to our donors — a common practice we refuse.
  • No prospecting. We do not use donor data for wealth research, capacity screening, or predictive modeling.
  • No advertising. We do not upload donor emails to ad platforms, do not run lookalike audiences, and do not engage in cross-context behavioral advertising.
  • No re-identification of anonymous donors. Anonymous remains anonymous.

Payment processors

We use Stripe (and PayPal when offered) because they do one thing exceptionally well: handle money without us seeing sensitive details. Stripe is PCI-DSS Service Provider Level 1 certified, the highest level. The processor collects what is needed for fraud prevention and settlement; we receive only the minimal fields described above. If Stripe flags a fraudulent attempt, we may receive a risk note but not additional sensitive data.

Any data Stripe holds is subject to Stripe’s privacy policy, not ours, but we contractually require that they use donor information only to process the transaction and prevent fraud — not for their own marketing to our donors.

Offline gifts and oral information

Our promise covers gifts made by check, wire, or in person, and information shared orally, in writing, or electronically. If you hand us a check after a hike, we do not add you to a database for sale. We deposit it, thank you, and keep the required record.

Your rights as a donor

You may, at any time:

  • Request access to what donor information we hold about you;
  • Request correction or deletion, subject to IRS seven-year record-keeping;
  • Request to be anonymous in any public listing (we default to no public listing anyway);
  • Opt out of any future non-receipt communications;
  • Request that we restrict use to tax receipt only.

We honor access, correction, deletion, and opt-out requests within 45 days, often much sooner. We will never discriminate against you for exercising these rights.

Reference — Donor Bill of Rights

The Association of Fundraising Professionals (AFP) Donor Bill of Rights affirms that philanthropy is built on trust. Donors have the right to know how gifts will be used, to receive appropriate acknowledgment, to assurance that information is handled with respect and confidentiality, and to expect that relationships with organizations are professional. We endorse and practice this Bill. You can read the full text via AFP.
