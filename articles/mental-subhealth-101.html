What is everyday mental sub-health?
An 8-question self-check.

Not a diagnosis. A cluster of common, early signals that your body and mind are asking for recovery — before burnout. We work in this window, especially for Bay Area workers, students, and young kids.

The gray zone is big

In the US, 73% adults report weekly stress symptoms (APA). Poor sleep latency, afternoon energy crash, irritability over small things, endless scrolling despite fatigue, Sunday evening dread, rumination at 2am, difficulty initiating tasks you used to enjoy, and feeling flat even after a weekend. Any 3 of these for 2+ weeks = everyday sub-health worth addressing with basic practices.

How we check — 8 questions

1) Sleep: Do you wake unrefreshed 3+ days/week? 2) Fog: Afternoon crash that coffee does not fix? 3) Scroll: Reaching for phone within 5 min of waking and before bed despite wanting not to? 4) Reactivity: Irritable with people you love over small things? 5) Rumination: Same worry loop at night? 6) Anhedonia-lite: Things that used to bring small joy now feel effortful? 7) Body: Headache, shoulder tension, stomach upset without medical cause? 8) Recovery: Weekend does not restore — Monday dread returns fast?

Why early matters

Behavioral activation and movement research shows habits formed early prevent escalation. Like dental floss — cheap daily practice prevents expensive later procedures. We teach regulation through movement, nature, and emotional language, not through purchasing products.

Bay Area context

Commute on 101, housing cost pressure, school application culture from elementary, tech on-call, wildfire smoke season limiting outdoor time — all amplify sub-health. Our response is local: walks in Presidio where fog filters light, redwood trails that lower physiological arousal, and community that does not require performance.

TRY TOMORROW • BAY AREA

3 small practices

  • Walk 30 min in Golden Gate Park or Presidio — notice breath and light, no podcast.
  • One no-work-talk walk this week in Redwood Regional or Rancho San Antonio — phone on Do Not Disturb.
  • Name one feeling to one person without fixing: “I feel overwhelmed and I am going to take three breaths.”
